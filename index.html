Abstract

Recently, 3D Gaussian splatting techniques have achieved significant progress in 3D reconstruction, owing to their efficient rendering speed and exceptional synthesis quality. However, existing methods still lack a theoretical exploration of the relationship between geometric information and reconstruction quality, resulting in an optimization process devoid of theoretical guidance and hindering substantial performance improvements. To address this issue, this paper proposes a depth-driven optimization framework. First, through rigorous mathematical derivation, we establish quantitative relationships between reconstruction accuracy and depth information under single-view and multi-view conditions, thereby identifying key geometric factors that influence reconstruction accuracy. Based on the theoretical analysis, a depth-difference adaptive normal constraint method is introduced for single-view reconstruction, which adaptively adjusts the constraint strength across different regions according to the depth differences. To further enhance reconstruction quality, we propose a cross-view depth consistency regularization strategy for multi-view settings, effectively strengthening geometric consistency across multiple views. Extensive experiments on public datasets, including DTU and Mip-NeRF360, demonstrate that our method achieves superior performance in both novel view synthesis and geometric reconstruction tasks, offering a more robust and efficient solution for 3D reconstruction.

Method

Overview: Initially, distance maps and normal maps are generated from sparse initial point clouds. Unbiased depth maps are then derived from these representations. These unbiased depth maps, together with the distance maps, are used to compute geometric reliability weights \( \mathcal{W}_{\Delta D} \), which in turn facilitate the formulation of adaptive normal constraints \( \mathcal{L}_{svgeo} \). Concurrently, coordinate transformations are applied to enforce depth consistency constraints \( \mathcal{L}_{mv\_D} \) across multiple views. High-precision global geometric reconstruction is ultimately achieved through a combination of single-view and multi-view geometric regularization.
